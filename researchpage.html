Samuel Franks Writing Style Guide

Undergraduate academic voice – tailored for you, Sam

Overview

This guide captures the distinctive writing voice of Samuel Franks, based on your style preferences and writing habits across papers. The guide is especially suited for longer research and academic essays.

Tone and Personality

Core Voice Characteristics

  • Earnest and straightforward: Your writing shows engagement with the topic without irony or showmanship.
  • Confident but measured: You present claims assertively, yet you admit complexity and limits of knowledge.
  • Conversational-academic: You balance formal academic requirements with language that still sounds like you.
  • Personally invested: You bring personal experience into the analysis (e.g., video games sharpening reflexes).
  • Faith/value-neutral unless assignment asks for faith: You don’t typically lean heavy into faith references unless required.

Emotional Register

  • Maintains a professional tone while letting your convictions appear: e.g., "I think", "I believe".
  • Shows intellectual humility: You’re okay with admitting “there are some outliers” or “it’s complicated”.
  • Shows concern when addressing issues (e.g., generational effects of gaming, societal issues).

Sentence Structure

Sentence Length Patterns

  • Average sentence length: long — many sentences are run-on, complex.
  • Mix: Some very long sentences, some shorter for effect.
  • Rhythm: Typically one long explanatory sentence, then a shorter emphatic one, then a transition.

Common Sentence Patterns

  • Opening statements:
    • “One crucial aspect of [topic] is [specific element]”
    • “[Topic] has always been an issue in [context], and with [circumstance], it is hard to [action].”
    • “[Stat] shows… and [related stat] reveals…”
  • Explanatory sequences:
    • “This means that [explanation]. [Consequence]. [Further implication].”
    • “When [condition], [result], so [implication].”
    • “Although [concession], [main point].”
  • Transitions:
    • “One thing that [verb] is [subject].”
    • “Another aspect is [point].”
    • “However, [contrast].”
  • Emphatic conclusions:
    • “This should [action].”
    • “Ultimately, [conclusive statement].”
    • “Overall, [summary point].”

Syntactic Preferences

  • Use subordinate clauses to add nuance.
  • Occasionally break parallelism for emphasis.
  • Sentence starters: varied; you’re comfortable with “Although”, “When”, “One thing that…”.
  • Passive voice: sparing; you prefer active voice.

Paragraph Structure

Paragraph Length

  • Body paragraphs: ~8-12 sentences (~150-250 words).
  • Introduction paragraphs: ~6-8 sentences, broad to specific.
  • Conclusion paragraphs: ~5-7 sentences, shorter and punchy.

Paragraph Organization: Standard Body Paragraph

  1. Topic sentence (often “One aspect…”).
  2. Context/explanation (1-2 sentences).
  3. Evidence/support (2-4 sentences).
  4. Analysis (2-3 sentences).
  5. Transition or mini-conclusion (connects to next paragraph).

Transition Strategies

  • Between paragraphs: echo key word from previous + new direction.
  • Within paragraphs: “However,” “This means that,” “Another” commonly used.
  • Section shifts: Can be abrupt (you noted “B” in answer for question 4 — more context and reasoning).

Common Phrases and Expressions

Frequently Used Transitions & Connectors

  • “One thing that…”
  • “Another aspect is…”
  • “This means that…”
  • “However,…”
  • “Although [concession], [main point]”
  • “Oftentimes…”
  • “All of these [elements] contribute to…”
  • “Overall,…”
  • “Ultimately,…”
  • “In order to [goal], [action] will need to [requirement].”

Evaluative & Analytical Phrases

  • “It’s important to note that…”
  • “One thing I understand better…”
  • “This will affect the way…”
  • “I would say…”
  • “Generally, most [group] agree…”
  • “There are some outliers…”
  • “After researching [topic], I have gained…”
  • “One crucial aspect of…”

Personal Reflection Phrases

  • “As a [student/person] I…”
  • “In my experience…”
  • “I feel like…”
  • “I think that…”
  • “I believe…”
  • “This has led me to believe…”

Hedging & Qualification

  • “Most likely”
  • “Probably”
  • “Oftentimes”
  • “Generally”
  • “It seems”
  • “Might/could/would”
  • “In some cases”

Word Choice & Vocabulary

Vocabulary Level

  • Accessible, direct language; not overloaded with jargon.
  • Use discipline-specific terms when appropriate.
  • Avoid over-complex words when simpler ones suffice.

Characteristic Word Choices

  • “Crucial”
  • “Oftentimes”
  • “Adverse”
  • “Aspects”
  • “Contributes to”
  • “Leads to”
  • “Overall”
  • “Ultimately”

Avoiding Repetition

  • Avoid starting too many paragraphs with “One aspect”.
  • Avoid repeating “This means that” too often — use alternatives like “As a result”, “Consequently”, “The implication is”.
  • Avoid “oftentimes” multiple times close together — vary with “frequently”, “commonly”, “in many cases”.

Formatting Preferences

Document Structure

  • Header: Name, Professor, Course, Date (day Month year).
  • Title: Centered, title case, not bolded.
  • Spacing: Double-spaced.
  • Indentation: First line of each paragraph indented.
  • Margins: 1 inch.
  • Font: Times New Roman (or equivalent serif) 12 pt.

Citation Style

  • Primary style: MLA.
  • In-text: Parenthetical with author or source.
  • Works Cited: Separate page, hanging indent, alphabetical.
  • Introduce sources with signal phrases (“According to…”, “[Author] states…”).
  • Prefer paraphrase over long block quotes; if quoting, integrate it and then explain.

Quotation Integration

  • Use “According to [Author]” or “[Source] states…” before quoting.
  • Short quote: integrate with signal phrase.
  • Block quotes: Avoid; you favor shorter integrated quotes.
  • After evidence: Explain significance — “This means that…” etc.

Argumentation & Rhetorical Strategies

Thesis Construction

  • Placed at end of introduction.
  • Style: Complex sentence with multiple clauses outlining 2-3 main supporting points.
  • Example: “Although [concession], [main claim] because/by [reason1], [reason2], and [reason3].”
  • Direct and specific rather than vague/broad.

Evidence Use

  • Types: Statistics, expert testimony, studies, personal experience.
  • Integration: Blend multiple types in a paragraph.
  • Source evaluation: Not deeply methodological critique but acknowledges limitations.
  • Personal experience: Used when relevant to establish relevance/credibility.

Counterargument Handling

  • Usually in the paragraph before conclusion.

    Persuasive Techniques

    • Logical appeals: Primary focus on reasoning and evidence.
    • Ethical appeals: Through personal experience, integrity, and perspective.
    • Emotional appeals: Subtle but present when human impact is discussed.
    • Use examples or scenarios: Tie concepts to real life (e.g., video games sharpening reflexes).

    Audience Awareness

    • Primary Audience: Undergraduate professors across disciplines.
    • Readers: College-level, general knowledge.
    • Values: Clear argument, research, personal insight.
    • Adaptation: Define terms when needed, provide context, explain significance of points.
    • Bridge disciplines: You may pull from theology, English, health/exercise science when relevant.

    Tone Calibration

    • For research papers: Slightly less personal pronouns, but "I" is allowed.
    • For reflections: More "I" statements and personal stories.
    • For article responses: Shorter, more direct, still maintains your voice.

    Subject-Specific Conventions

    • Theology Papers: Insert belief statements respectfully when relevant. Use scripture if required and properly reference. Show connections to life/practice. Example vocabulary: "Trinity", "monotheism", "Tritheism".
    • English Composition Papers: Clear thesis, developed arguments, proper source integration, address multiple views, engage contemporary issues.
    • Health/Exercise Science or Honors English: Use discipline terms when needed but keep accessible. Blend evidence with implications. Ground discussion with personal experience.

    Structural Patterns

    • Aspect Breakdown Pattern: Introduce concept, Body P1 "One aspect is…", Body P2 "Another aspect is…", Body P3 or counterargument, Conclusion: synthesize.
    • Problem-Solution Pattern: Introduction: problem, Body1: depth, Body2: causes/existing approaches, Body3: solution or argument for action, Counterargument paragraph, Conclusion: urgency + feasibility.
    • Personal Journey Pattern: Intro: personal connection, Body: new info/analysis, Show change in thinking, Conclusion: changed understanding + future application.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    • Starting too many paragraphs with “One aspect” — vary phrasing.
    • Using “oftentimes” repeatedly.
    • Too many long or run-on sentences without breaks — use long sentences intentionally but not uncontrollably.
    • Layering in too much jargon when plain language works.
    • Leaving personal anecdotes disconnected from analysis.
    • Over-relying on “This means that” — switch to alternatives.

    Final Notes

    • Strengths of This Style
      • Your voice is genuine and relatable while still academic.
      • You bring personal relevance which makes essays stand out.
      • Your tone balances formal and conversational effectively.
    • Areas for Growth
      • Manage run-ons: Use long sentences for effect but anchor with shorter ones.
      • Vary transitional starters beyond “One aspect” and “Another”.
      • Ensure every personal anecdote ties back to the analysis.
      • Balance personal voice with research-heavy content.
    • Evolution Over Time
      • Voice is maturing: earlier essays were more informal, broad claims; now structure arguments and integrate evidence better.
      • Core voice—earnest, personally invested, straightforward—remains strong.
