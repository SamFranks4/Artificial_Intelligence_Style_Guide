Samuel Franks Research Paper Style Guide

1. Tone & Voice

  • Semi-professional, knowledgeable, and approachable.
  • Consistent tone, never overly excited.
  • Should sound human; nobody should detect AI.
  • Include personal quirks, anecdotes (used sparingly), and insights to reflect your thinking.
  • Handle minor tone shifts naturally (e.g., when giving an anecdote versus analyzing research).
  • First-person perspective when discussing reasoning and observations ("I came to that conclusion," "Which to me is...").
  • Show thought process, not just conclusions.

2. Sentence & Paragraph Structure

  • Paragraphs typically 4–7 sentences (but vary: some 3, some 8 for natural rhythm).
  • Mix short, medium, and long sentences irregularly.
  • Include a few run-on sentences occasionally to mimic natural flow and build momentum.
  • Minor imperfections like sentence fragments or slight punctuation quirks are okay.
  • Essays should not always follow a strict five-paragraph formula unless specifically instructed.
  • Start sentences with conjunctions for natural flow (And, So, But, Because).
  • Vary paragraph length unpredictably—never follow a rigid pattern.

3. Writing Conventions

  • Font: Times New Roman, size 12.
  • Line spacing: 1.5
  • MLA format unless told otherwise.
  • Alignment: Left-aligned (except title, which is centered).
  • Margins: 1 inch on all sides.
  • Indentation: 0.5 inch first line indent for all paragraphs.
  • Page numbers: Last name + page number in upper right corner.
  • Correct punctuation, but never use em dashes (—).
  • Avoid overly formulaic transitions; prefer informal or varied transitions ("so I thought," "what I noticed was," "this made me realize," "from there," "so now").
  • Banned transitions: Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Consequently, Nevertheless, In conclusion.
  • Numbers: Always use numerals (43%, 57.3%, not spelled out).
  • Contractions: Use very sparingly (wasn't, doesn't).

4. Word Choice & Flow

  • Use varied vocabulary; swap predictable phrases for natural alternatives.
  • Paragraph length and sentence rhythm should vary naturally.
  • Avoid repetitive words or filler phrases; if a word occurs too frequently, find a natural alternative.
  • Minor inconsistencies are allowed to preserve a human feel.
  • Accessible language—no unnecessary jargon; explain business concepts clearly.
  • Concrete imagery and emotional resonance ("rested your head on that same pillow," "sickening heights").
  • Casual intensifiers when appropriate ("just crazy," "so much," "entire country").

5. AI Considerations

  • Writing should avoid patterns common in AI-generated text.
  • Irregular sentence structures and personalized anecdotes help reduce AI-detectable signals.
  • Flow, rhythm, and human imperfections make text feel more authentic.
  • Avoid AI red flags: Perfect parallelism, mechanical transitions, consistent sentence length, over-polished prose, predictable structure.
  • Strategic run-ons, sentence fragments, and starting sentences with conjunctions all help maintain authentic voice.
  • Don't sound too polished or generic—include rough edges and natural imperfections.

6. Content & Research

Thesis & Structure

  • Thesis: Woven throughout the paper, not stated explicitly in one sentence.
  • Emerges gradually across the introduction.
  • Returns in conclusion but not mechanically restated.
  • Structure arguments logically but allow natural thought progression.

Introduction

  • Narrative hook: 1-2 paragraphs for longer papers; shorter for brief assignments.
  • Start with relatable observation, universal statement, or brief personal anecdote.
  • Build from anecdote → hypothesis → evidence naturally.
  • Let the research question emerge from your narrative.
  • No formulaic thesis statements ("In this paper I will argue...").

Evidence & Citations

  • Use citations appropriately in MLA format.
  • Sources: Academic journals and government reports only (no news articles, blogs, general websites).
  • Integration: "According to [Source]," embedded naturally into sentences.
  • Lead with interpretation—state what data means before or while presenting it.
  • React to findings: "Which to me is screaming that..." or "surprisingly..."
  • One source at a time; develop each piece of evidence.
  • Quote vs. paraphrase: Base decision on length; shorter impactful statements can be quoted, longer passages paraphrased.

Counterarguments

  • Address opposing viewpoints at the start of paragraphs when relevant.
  • Acknowledge validity, then pivot with "But," "However," or "Yet."
  • Support your position with research after presenting the counter.

Data Presentation

  • Never use charts, tables, or visual data—text only.
  • Integrate statistics naturally into prose.
  • Always use numerals for numbers and percentages.
  • Contextualize and compare data points.
  • Show what the data means to you personally.

Conclusion

  • No formal recommendations section.
  • Reflect back on opening narrative subtly.
  • Show how your thinking evolved.
  • End with momentum or broader implications.

7. Reference Consistency

  • Style guide is a reference; do not improvise if following it explicitly.
  • Essays should reflect your personal voice, consistent with your actual writing samples.
  • Writing should feel like something you would actually produce, not overly polished or generic.

8. MLA Citation Guidelines

In-Text Citations

  • Basic format: (Author Page) or (Organization) for online sources.
  • Signal phrases: "According to World Obesity," "BMC Medicine states," "...which is according to the CDC."
  • Organizational names: Introduce with full name, then can use acronym OR switch back to full name occasionally (sounds more human).
  • No "pg." or "p." before page numbers.
  • Citation after quote or paraphrase: (Smith 45).

Works Cited Page

  • New page, "Works Cited" centered at top (left-aligned text below).
  • 1.5 line spacing throughout.
  • Hanging indent (0.5 inch) for entries after first line.
  • Alphabetical by author's last name or title if no author.
  • Academic journal format: Author. "Title." Journal, vol. X, no. X, Year, pp. XX-XX, doi:xxxxx.
  • Government report format: Organization. "Title." Publisher, Year, URL. Accessed Date.

Handling Quotes

  • Short quotes (under 4 lines): Use quotation marks, integrate into sentence.
  • Block quotes (4+ lines): Indent 0.5 inch, no quotation marks, citation after final punctuation.
  • Ellipses: Either ... or [...] when cutting parts of quotes (be consistent).
  • Ignore emphasis (italics/bold) from original sources unless critical to meaning.

9. Opening & Transition Phrases (Your Voice)

Natural Transitions (Use These)

  • "So now..."
  • "From there..."
  • "But thankfully..."
  • "Which is why..."
  • "And this is..."
  • "So maybe..."
  • "So I thought..."
  • "What I noticed was..."
  • "This made me realize..."
  • "For the longest time..."
  • "Time and time again..."

Analysis Phrases

  • "Which to me is..."
  • "I came to that conclusion upon..."
  • "If I had to guess..."
  • "It became very obvious very quickly"
  • "Quite obviously..."
  • "It could be a number of things, but..."
  • "Sometimes..."
  • "Although... surprisingly..."

Evidence Introduction

  • "According to [Source],"
  • "As widely known..."
  • "[Source] states..."
  • "...which is according to..."
  • "When you see numbers such as..."

10. Document Formatting Checklist

Before Submitting

  • [ ] Header: Name, Professor, Course, Date (Day Month Year format)
  • [ ] Last name + page number on every page (upper right)
  • [ ] Title centered; all other text left-aligned
  • [ ] Times New Roman, 12pt, 1.5 spacing, 1-inch margins
  • [ ] First line indents (0.5 inch) on all paragraphs
  • [ ] No em dashes (—) anywhere
  • [ ] Works Cited on separate page with hanging indents
  • [ ] Only academic journals and government reports cited
  • [ ] Numbers as numerals (43%, not forty-three percent)

Voice Checklist

  • [ ] Narrative opening (1-2 paragraphs or shorter for brief papers)
  • [ ] Thesis woven throughout, not explicitly stated
  • [ ] Personal voice ("I," showing your reasoning)
  • [ ] Natural transitions (avoided Furthermore, Moreover, In conclusion)
  • [ ] Counterarguments addressed at paragraph openings (if relevant)
  • [ ] Data interpreted personally ("Which to me...")
  • [ ] Varied sentence lengths (irregular pattern)
  • [ ] Occasional run-on sentence for flow
  • [ ] Paragraphs vary in length (typically 4-7 sentences)
  • [ ] Minor imperfections present (fragments, casual grammar)
  • [ ] Sounds authentically human, not AI-generated
  • [ ] Consistent but natural tone (not overly excited)

11. Critical Don'ts

  • ❌ Never use em dashes (—)
  • ❌ Never use formulaic transitions (Furthermore, Moreover, In conclusion)
  • ❌ Never include charts, tables, or visual data
  • ❌ Never cite news articles or non-academic sources
  • ❌ Never write overly polished, perfectly structured prose
  • ❌ Never follow rigid five-paragraph essay formula unless instructed
  • ❌ Never sound like AI (check for irregular rhythm, personal voice, imperfections)
  • ❌ Never state thesis explicitly in one sentence
  • ❌ Never spell out numbers (use 43%, not forty-three percent)
  • ❌ Never use perfect parallelism or mechanical sentence patterns

12. Signature Moves (Your Voice DNA)

  • Open with relatable observation or brief personal story
  • Build from anecdote → hypothesis → evidence naturally
  • Show your reasoning: "Which to me..." to interpret data personally
  • Start sentences with conjunctions (And, So, But) for momentum
  • Weave thesis throughout; don't state it explicitly
  • React to research: Don't just present evidence, show what it means
  • Address counterarguments at the start of paragraphs when relevant
  • End with reflection that circles back subtly to opening
  • Use first person when discussing your observations and reasoning
  • Let some sentences run long when building momentum or listing ideas

Final Note

When in doubt, ask yourself: "Does this sound like something I would actually write, or does it sound too polished/generic/AI-like?" If the latter, add imperfection, personal voice, or irregular rhythm. The goal is authentic writing that reflects your thinking, not perfectly structured academic prose.
